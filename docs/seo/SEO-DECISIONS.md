# Landmark Flooring — SEO Decisions & Sources

> Verified positions taken during the page-plan research. Each was adversarially fact-checked against current Google guidance before being baked into the plan.

## Adversarial verification verdicts

### [PARTIALLY-SUPPORTED] For an 8-city service area served from a single Carson City showroom, building a separate keyword-targeted landing page for each city is a sound SEO strategy and not a Google doorway-page violation if the pages are made genuinely unique.
**Reasoning:** The claim is directionally defensible at this small scale (8 cities, not 800), but its core framing is wrong and dangerously incomplete, so it cannot be called "supported."

WHAT'S RIGHT: At 8 cities from one Carson City showroom, a page-per-city architecture is within mainstream local-SEO practice. The doorway-page enforcement Google describes (and that John Mueller publicly warned about) targets SCALE and INTENT — Mueller's cited cautionary case was building out ~1,300 city pages purely to rank for "[keyword] + [city]" (seroundtable.com; localsearchforum.com). 8 hand-built pages is two orders of magnitude away from that profile. Flooring-industry guidance (fourarrowsmarketing.com) explicitly recommends dedicated pages for the major towns/cities in a contractor's 20-30 mile radius. So the strategy itself is sound IF executed well.

WHY IT'S ONLY PARTIALLY SUPPORTED — the claim's logic is flawed in three ways:

1) "Genuinely unique" is NECESSARY BUT NOT SUFFICIENT, and the claim treats it as the whole test. Google's actual spam policy defines doorways by PURPOSE and USER VALUE, not by duplication: "Sites or pages created to rank for specific, similar search queries... created purely to capture search engine traffic but not add value for users" — including "pages targeted at specific regions or cities that funnel users to one page" (developers.google.com/search/docs/essentials/spam-policies). You can write 8 textually distinct, non-duplicate pages and STILL trip the doorway test if their only real purpose is keyword capture and they funnel everyone back to the same showroom/quote form without adding location-specific value. Post-2024, this is also policed by the Scaled Content Abuse policy and SpamBrain, with enforcement tightening through the March 2024 core update and Feb 2025 update (breaklineagency.com; metaflow.life). "Unique" in Google's eyes means genuinely DIFFERENT VALUE per page (local projects, local reviews/testimonials, regional product/subfloor/climate considerations, real photos), not spun synonyms or city-name swaps.

2) The claim omits the most important strategic fact for a SINGLE-location SAB: city pages mainly help ORGANIC rankings, not the Map Pack. A 2024 Whitespark controlled test found that defined service areas do NOT influence Maps rankings — proximity to the verified physical address governs the local pack — and Sterling Sky found "Is a Service Area Business" negatively correlated with ranking for "near me" (sterlingsky.ca; whitespark.ca). So Landmark should NOT expect these 8 pages to win the Map Pack in cities beyond Carson City; they compete in the organic ("ten blue links") results below the map. Selling per-city pages as a route into the local pack for all 8 cities would be a false promise.

3) Risk is proportional to honesty of presence. Pages for cities where the business has done real work are low-risk and effective; pages for cities it merely "would serve" but has no projects, reviews, or genuine footprint in are exactly the thin/funnel pattern Google flags (ricketyroo.com; brightlocal.com).

So: sound architecture at this scale, but the "if genuinely unique" exemption as stated is a misreading of the policy (intent + user value is the real test), and the claim ignores the Map-Pack-vs-organic reality that determines whether the pages even pay off.

**What we actually do:** Build the 8 city pages — but treat them as ORGANIC-search assets, not a Map Pack shortcut, and earn their uniqueness rather than asserting it.

1) Right scale, right method: 8 hand-built pages is far below the doorway/scaled-content threshold Mueller warned about (~1,300 pages). The risk isn't the count; it's templated thinness. Never produce them by city-name find-and-replace on one master template.

2) Pass Google's ACTUAL doorway test (purpose + user value, not just non-duplication). Each city page must carry location-specific substance Google and users can verify: real completed projects in/near that city (with photos and addresses to the neighborhood level), genuine customer reviews/testimonials from that city, regionally relevant detail (e.g., subfloor/moisture/climate considerations, popular local product lines, HOA or new-build patterns), driving/service logistics from the Carson City showroom, and city-specific FAQs. If a page has none of this and just funnels to the same quote form, it IS a doorway regardless of how "unique" the prose reads.

3) Set correct expectations: city pages move ORGANIC rankings for "[service] + [city]" queries, not the Google Maps/local pack — the showroom's Carson City address governs proximity in the pack (Whitespark 2024 test; Sterling Sky 2025). Don't promise pack visibility in the 7 outer cities. The pack play is your verified Carson City GBP plus categories, services, and review velocity.

4) Be honest about footprint. Prioritize and build the strongest pages for cities where Landmark has actually worked. For cities with little/no real presence, either delay the page until you have real proof to put on it, or consolidate weaker areas into a richer regional page rather than shipping a thin stub.

5) Internal linking and GBP tie-in: link each city page from a clear service-area hub (avoid orphan pages — a common doorway-detection trigger per Orbit Media), and where relevant link from the GBP, which research ties to local visibility.

6) Quality bar over keyword targeting: write for the buyer, not the query string. Avoid keyword-stuffed title/H1 patterns. The pages that survive core updates read like a local company genuinely documenting its work in that city.

Bottom line: greenlight the 8 pages, but reframe the rationale — uniqueness alone doesn't grant a doorway exemption; demonstrable per-city user value does, and the payoff is organic, not Map Pack.

### [REFUTED] FAQPage structured data still earns FAQ rich results in Google for a local flooring business in 2025-2026.
**Reasoning:** A local flooring business has had no realistic path to FAQ rich results since August 2023, and as of 2026 FAQ rich results are gone entirely for everyone. Timeline from authoritative sources: (1) In its August 2023 Search Central blog post ("Changes to HowTo and FAQ rich results"), Google announced it was limiting FAQ rich results to "well-known, authoritative government and health websites" — for all other sites the rich result would no longer regularly show. A local flooring contractor is the textbook example of a site that fell OUTSIDE this carve-out, so FAQ rich results were effectively unavailable to it for nearly three years. (2) On May 7, 2026, Google deprecated FAQ rich results entirely — they stopped appearing in Search even for the government/health publishers that had been preserved. Google added a note to the top of its FAQPage structured-data documentation, will remove the FAQ search-appearance filter, the rich-result report, and Rich Results Test support in June 2026, and the Search Console API will stop returning FAQ data in August 2026 (corroborated by Search Engine Journal, Search Engine Land, Search Engine Roundtable). So for the entire 2025-2026 window in the claim, a local flooring business could NOT earn FAQ rich results: throughout 2025 it was excluded by the 2023 authoritative-sites restriction, and from May 2026 the feature does not exist at all. The claim is false. Important nuance kept separate: the FAQPage MARKUP (schema.org type) is still valid and harmless to leave on a page — but valid markup is not the same as earning a rich result, which is the specific thing the claim asserts.

**What we actually do:** Do NOT add FAQPage schema to a local flooring site expecting FAQ rich results — they are unavailable. Local/commercial businesses were cut off from FAQ rich results in August 2023 (Google restricted them to well-known authoritative government and health sites), and Google fully deprecated FAQ rich results for everyone on May 7, 2026 (filter, report, and Rich Results Test support removed June 2026; Search Console API FAQ data ends August 2026). What a flooring SEO should actually do: (1) Keep writing genuinely useful FAQ CONTENT on service and location pages (pricing, install timelines, service areas, warranties, hardwood vs LVP vs tile, financing) — the value is user trust and topical coverage, plus eligibility for AI Overviews / AI-assistant citations and Bing/Perplexity/other RAG crawlers, which still parse FAQ content. (2) You can leave existing FAQPage markup in place — Google says unused structured data does not cause problems — but it is no longer earning any Google SERP feature, so there is no point adding it solely for that. Plain, well-structured HTML Q&A (clear question headings + concise answers) is sufficient for AI/answer engines. (3) Redirect structured-data effort toward types Google STILL supports and rewards for local flooring: LocalBusiness (or HomeAndConstructionBusiness), Service, Product/Offer with Review/AggregateRating where legitimately earned, BreadcrumbList, and Organization. (4) Prioritize the things that actually move local flooring rankings: a complete, accurate Google Business Profile, NAP consistency, genuine reviews, location/service landing pages, and Core Web Vitals — not FAQ rich-result chasing.

### [PARTIALLY-SUPPORTED] "Flooring store near me" / "[material] near me" style queries are higher-volume and higher-priority for a local flooring retailer than informational guide keywords.
**Reasoning:** The claim bundles two assertions that the 2024-2026 evidence treats very differently. PRIORITY (strongly supported): "near me"/local-intent queries trigger the Maps/Local Pack roughly 100% of the time and essentially never trigger an AI Overview, so the click is capturable and the intent is bottom-of-funnel — BrightLocal/industry data put local-search purchase rates near 78%, and Sterling Sky's 2026 outlook frames transactional local visibility as the core revenue driver. Meanwhile informational guide content is precisely what is being cannibalized: ~88% of AI Overview triggers are informational queries, zero-click searches rose from 56% to 69% (May 2024->May 2025), and informational/non-branded CTR fell ~20% (Amsive) up to ~61% on AIO queries. So as a priority for lead capture and ROI in 2026, near-me/transactional clearly outranks informational guides. VOLUME (not reliably true, partly refuted): the "higher-volume" half is the weak link. In flooring specifically, broad informational head terms ("types of hardwood flooring," "hardwood vs laminate," "flooring installation cost," "best flooring for kitchens") frequently carry equal or GREATER raw monthly search volume than "[material] near me," which tends to be lower-volume but extremely high-intent. "Near me" usage is growing (46% of consumers add it, growth outpacing general search), but the real edge is intent and SERP-feature capturability, not raw volume. The claim also over-dismisses informational content, which still drives top-of-funnel discovery and is the main path to being cited in AI Overviews/AI answers — increasingly a discovery channel for local services. So: right on priority, unreliable/overstated on volume, and wrong to treat informational keywords as low-value.

**What we actually do:** For a local flooring retailer in 2026, prioritize near-me/local transactional queries — "flooring store near me," "[material] flooring [city]," "[material] installation near me" — but base that priority on INTENT and SERP-capturability, not on an assumption that they have higher search volume. Why: these queries reliably surface the Google Maps/Local Pack (~100% for "near me") and almost never trigger AI Overviews, so the click is winnable and converts (local searches purchase at ~78%). Concretely: (1) Nail the Google Business Profile, NAP consistency, reviews, and proximity/relevance signals that drive Local Pack ranking. (2) Build dedicated location + material/service landing pages ("[material] flooring in [city]") — the "material + location" formula — since each serveable combination is a transactional page worth ranking. (3) Do NOT abandon informational guides: many flooring head terms ("hardwood vs laminate," "flooring cost per square foot," "best flooring for pets/kitchens") have high volume and now feed AI Overviews and brand discovery. Treat them as top-of-funnel: structure them to be cited/quoted by AI Overviews (clear headings, FAQ schema, original data/photos, strong entity signals), accept they will drive fewer raw clicks due to zero-click, and use them to build authority and capture demand earlier — then funnel toward the transactional pages. (4) Validate actual volumes per market with a keyword tool (Semrush/Ahrefs/GBP insights) rather than assuming near-me wins on volume; in many flooring markets informational terms out-volume near-me terms while converting worse. Net: near-me/transactional = highest priority for revenue; informational = still valuable, repositioned for AI-era discovery rather than direct clicks.

### [SUPPORTED] Dedicated per-material product/service pages (carpet, luxury vinyl, laminate, hardwood) rank better than a single combined products section on the homepage.
**Reasoning:** Multiple current authoritative sources back the directional claim, and I found no credible 2024-2026 source arguing the opposite for a multi-service/multi-product local business. Whitespark's 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors report (the largest practitioner survey in local SEO) ranks "Dedicated Page for Each Service" as the #1 factor for Local Organic rankings (and #2 for AI/LLM visibility) — this is the single strongest data point and it directly maps to per-material flooring pages. The mechanism is well established and consistent with Google's own guidance: a page targeting one specific intent (e.g., "luxury vinyl plank flooring [city]") can rank for that query, whereas a combined homepage section dilutes topical focus, gives each material only a few sentences of depth, and forces Google to disambiguate many competing keywords on one URL. Google's John Mueller has repeatedly favored a hierarchical theme-pyramid structure (broad category -> specific child pages) over flat single-page structures for anything beyond a tiny site, and notes that thin coverage of a topic loses to comprehensive coverage. Flooring-specific guides (Four Arrows, Shawn the SEO Geek) explicitly state a generic combined "products/services" section does not rank for specific material searches, and recommend a dedicated page per flooring type. HOWEVER, the claim is not unconditional — the advantage only holds if each dedicated page carries genuinely unique, substantial content (commonly cited ~600-800+ words, real specifics: brands carried, pros/cons, pricing ranges, install process, photos, FAQs). The same sources warn that the failure mode is thin/near-duplicate pages, which Google can treat as low-value or doorway pages and which can rank WORSE than one strong combined page. So separate pages "rank better" is conditional on content depth and differentiation, not automatic from URL count alone. I could not load Whitespark's page directly (403 on WebFetch), but the #1-factor finding was corroborated across two independent WebSearch summaries citing the same 2026 report, so I'm treating it as reliable rather than uncertain.

**What we actually do:** For a flooring company, build a dedicated, substantive page for each flooring material you sell/install — carpet, luxury vinyl (LVP/LVT), laminate, hardwood, plus tile/engineered wood if offered — rather than a single combined products block on the homepage. This is well-supported and is currently the top-ranked local-organic factor in Whitespark's 2026 survey. To actually realize the ranking gain (not just create more URLs): (1) Make each page genuinely unique and deep — roughly 600-1,000+ words covering that material's pros/cons, durability/water-resistance, room suitability, brands you carry, price ranges, install/timeline, care, and an FAQ; never spin near-duplicate templates with only the material name swapped (that risks thin-content/doorway treatment and can rank worse than one good page). (2) Give each page a clear, intent-matched title/H1 and URL (e.g., /flooring/luxury-vinyl-plank/), unique meta description, and original photos of your own jobs. (3) Keep the homepage as a hub that links out to each material page (theme-pyramid hierarchy with strong internal linking — per Mueller), so the homepage still earns brand/"flooring [city]" queries while children capture material-specific queries. (4) For local intent, the highest-value pattern is material x location pages (e.g., "hardwood flooring installation [city]") layered on top of the core material pages — but only where you can write authentic local content; do not mass-generate city pages. (5) Add Product/Service or relevant structured data and link material pages to related project/gallery and review content. Net: separate pages win, but the win comes from depth, differentiation, and clean internal-linking architecture — not from page count.

### [PARTIALLY-SUPPORTED] Adding a blog/resource hub of buying guides materially helps a local flooring store rank for its transactional money-page keywords.
**Reasoning:** The claim is true in a narrow, indirect sense but is overstated as written, and it is outright wrong for the part of "transactional money-page" rankings that flooring stores care about most: the local pack / Google Maps and "near me" results. The evidence splits along two ranking surfaces.

(1) LOCAL PACK (the 3-pack / Maps): Multiple authoritative studies say blog buying guides do essentially NOTHING here. Sterling Sky's 2025 study of 8,186 businesses across 200 cities found that even adding location-relevant phrasing to page content "didn't move the needle at all for local pack rankings," and only a slight correlation exists between substantive on-page word count and local rank. Whitespark's and BrightLocal's local ranking factor analyses put proximity, Google Business Profile signals, review quantity/velocity/keywords, and citation/NAP consistency as the dominant local-pack factors, with on-page content a minor and DECLINING share (BrightLocal notes on-page fell from ~19% in 2023). Crucially, the on-page weight that does exist comes from the money page / service page itself (relevance signals on the page being ranked), not from a separate blog hub. So for transactional local-pack queries like "flooring store near me," a buying-guide hub is not a material lever.

(2) LOCAL ORGANIC (classic blue-link results below the map): Here the claim has limited support, but ONLY through indirect mechanisms, not direct ranking transfer. A buying-guide hub can help money pages by (a) supplying internal links with descriptive anchor text that pass authority and relevance to the service/money page, (b) earning links and building site-level/topical authority within the flooring topic, and (c) capturing top-of-funnel research traffic ("luxury vinyl vs hardwood," "best flooring for kitchens"). But topical-authority guidance (Google's helpful-content/E-E-A-T framing and current SEO consensus) is explicit that relevance benefit accrues within a tightly related topic cluster and via internal linking and earned authority — it is NOT a generic "publish guides and your money pages rise" effect. The flooring-vertical sources themselves consistently classify buying guides as awareness/research-stage content that "might not convert immediately" and is valuable for trust, brand, lead capture, and AI Overview/citation visibility — explicitly distinguishing it from the dedicated service and location pages that actually do the transactional ranking.

The word "materially" is the failure point. The material driver of money-page rankings is the money page itself plus, for local intent, GBP + reviews + citations + proximity. A blog hub is a supporting, second-order asset whose effect on transactional rankings is indirect, modest, and contingent on internal linking and topical relevance — and near-zero for the local pack. So: supported as a minor indirect contributor to organic money-page rankings; refuted as a material or primary lever, and largely irrelevant to local-pack transactional rankings.

**What we actually do:** Do NOT build a buying-guide hub expecting it to materially lift your transactional money-page (e.g. "hardwood flooring installation [city]," "flooring store near me") rankings — especially in the local pack/Maps, where studies show content barely matters. Sequence and weight your effort as follows:

1) Win the transactional rankings first with the assets that actually move them: (a) a dedicated, genuinely unique service page per material/service (hardwood, LVP, tile, carpet, laminate, refinishing) and per primary city/service-area, with real relevance signals on the page itself; (b) a fully optimized Google Business Profile (correct primary category, services, photos, posts); (c) review quantity AND recent velocity with natural keyword/location mentions; (d) consistent NAP citations. These — plus proximity — are the dominant local-pack/"near me" factors per Sterling Sky, Whitespark, and BrightLocal.

2) Treat a buying-guide / resource hub as a SUPPORTING, top-of-funnel asset, not a money-page ranking tactic. Its legitimate jobs are: capturing research-stage searches ("LVP vs hardwood," "best flooring for kitchens"), earning links, demonstrating expertise/E-E-A-T, fueling AI Overview/AI-search citations, and feeding leads into the funnel.

3) If you want the hub to indirectly help money pages, engineer the linking: every guide should internally link to the relevant service/location (money) page with descriptive anchor text, keep the cluster tightly on the flooring topic (avoid unrelated topics, which dilute topical signals), and use the guides to attract the backlinks that lift the whole domain. The benefit flows through internal links + earned authority + topical relevance — it is not automatic from publishing.

4) Measure it as awareness/lead-gen and assisted conversions, not as a money-keyword ranking lever. Don't divert budget from GBP, reviews, citations, and service/location pages to a content hub on the theory that guides will rank your transactional pages — that theory is not supported by current local-search data.

### [PARTIALLY-SUPPORTED] Generic local head terms dominate flooring search: 'flooring store near me', 'carpet stores near me', and 'flooring installation near me' are the highest-demand, highest-intent queries, and they trigger the Google Maps 'map pack' that drives an estimated 40-60% of a flooring contractor's lead volume (per Four Arrows Marketing and other flooring-SEO authorities).
**Reasoning:** The claim bundles three sub-claims of decreasing reliability. (1) "Near me" queries are high-intent: SUPPORTED. Commercial "near me" terms are genuinely high commercial-intent, and the map pack does capture a large share of clicks on local-intent SERPs (Backlinko: 42% of clicks go to the Map Pack; BrightLocal Local Services Ads study: local pack ~32% of clicks, top-3 ~44%). (2) These specific generic head terms are the "highest-demand, highest-intent" queries to target: WEAK/MISLEADING. Authoritative flooring-SEO and local-SEO guidance is the opposite — generic single head terms are hyper-competitive and lower-converting, while specific material+city long-tail (e.g., "vinyl plank installation [city]", "hardwood floor refinishing [city]") convert far better (cited 6-15% vs generic). More importantly, "near me" is a behavioral modifier Google appends based on user location; you do not "target" or "trigger the map pack" by stuffing "near me" into pages. Sterling Sky's 2025 study (8,186 businesses, 200 cities) found that adding "near me/near you" text did NOT move local-pack rankings — proximity, Google Business Profile completeness, categories, and reviews drive map-pack placement. (3) The "map pack drives 40-60% of a flooring contractor's lead volume, per Four Arrows Marketing": REFUTED as stated. No published study supports a precise "40-60% of total lead volume" figure; it is an unsourced agency-blog assertion (e.g., datapins.com, BizIQ), not original Four Arrows research, and conflates "share of clicks on local SERPs" (a CTR metric, ~32-44%) with "share of a contractor's total leads across all channels" (which also includes referrals, paid, GMB direct, organic, repeat). The "40-60%" string actually appears in current sources describing referral-lead conversion lift, not map-pack lead share. Finally, the premise is increasingly stale: through 2025 Google rolled out AI local packs (1-2 businesses instead of 3, call buttons stripped) and grew local-pack ad units from ~1% to ~22% of queries and LSAs from 11% to 31% (Whitespark/Sterling Sky Q1 2025+), eroding organic map-pack share rather than locking in a stable 40-60%.

**What we actually do:** For a flooring contractor's SEO in 2026: (1) Treat the map pack as important but not a fixed 40-60% lead engine — it captures roughly a third to mid-40s percent of CLICKS on local-intent searches (Backlinko 42%, BrightLocal ~32%, top-3 ~44%), and that share is shrinking as AI packs and paid ad units expand. Track your own call/form attribution rather than citing a borrowed "40-60%." (2) You don't rank for "near me" by writing "near me" — it's location-personalized. To win those impressions, optimize the Google Business Profile (correct primary category like "Flooring contractor"/"Carpet store," complete services/products, photos, hours), build review velocity and ratings (which measurably lift pack CTR), maintain NAP/citation consistency, and improve proximity-adjacent signals (service-area pages, local relevance). (3) Keyword strategy: don't bet the site on a handful of generic head terms ("flooring store near me"). Build a page architecture around specific material + service + city/neighborhood long-tail ("luxury vinyl plank installation [city]," "carpet store [city]," "hardwood floor refinishing [neighborhood]") — lower volume but far higher conversion and far less competition — plus city/service landing pages to feed both organic and the pack. (4) Plan for diversification: with AI local packs stripping call buttons and showing fewer businesses, and LSAs/ads taking SERP space, pair organic/local-pack work with Local Services Ads, reviews, and referral programs rather than treating the 3-pack as a guaranteed lead majority.

URL like yourwebsite.com/material-city"). Four Arrows, Gushwork, and Loopex similarly push per-city location pages plus per-service/material pages. (2) Google's March 2024 SCALED CONTENT ABUSE policy explicitly names "data-template pages that swap location names, product names, or keyword variants into identical page structures" as a violation - which is exactly what an unconstrained material×city matrix produces (e.g., 8 materials × 25 cities = 200 near-duplicate pages). ## Structured-data strategy

Build ONE site-wide JSON-LD @id graph centered on a single canonical business node. Because Landmark is a flooring store + contractor with one physical showroom, type it as LocalBusiness using the most specific subtype HomeAndConstructionBusiness (Google explicitly recommends the most specific LocalBusiness subtype; HomeAndConstructionBusiness is the correct one for a flooring store/installer). Give it a stable, unchanging @id (e.g. "https://landmarkflooringusa.com/#business") and populate name, address (PostalAddress: 2085 East William St, Suite 10, Carson City, NV 89701), telephone "+1-775-297-3236", url, logo, image (real photos), geo (GeoCoordinates), openingHoursSpecification (Mo–Fr 09:00–17:00), priceRange, and areaServed (the 8 cities + "Northern Nevada"). Put this node site-wide (footer/template) so every page references the same @id. Add a WebSite node ("#website") on the homepage. WIRING: Service and Product nodes on money pages reference the business via provider: {"@id": ".../#business"}; BreadcrumbList nodes reference the page; this avoids duplicating business details on every page. SERVICE vs PRODUCT: default to Service for installation pages (serviceType + provider + areaServed) because Landmark primarily sells a sold-and-installed service and Service is not tied to a rich result but still feeds search + AI understanding. Use Product (or multi-type Product+Service) ONLY where a page genuinely shows a product image plus a real price/offer, since the Product rich result requires image and price/review (per SEJ/Google). Do NOT slap Product schema on service-only pages. BREADCRUMBLIST: add BreadcrumbList to every non-home page to reinforce the /flooring/<material>/, /services/<service>/, /flooring/<city>/ hierarchy (this is one of the few schema types here that can still produce a visible breadcrumb in SERPs). FAQPAGE — CURRENT REALITY: Google restricted FAQ rich results in Aug 2023 to only well-known government and health sites; for a flooring business FAQ markup will NOT render star/accordion rich results. It is still worth adding because Google says it continues to use FAQ structured data to understand pages, and it feeds AI/answer engines — but set expectations: it is a comprehension aid, not a SERP-feature play. Keep FAQ content visible on-page regardless. REVIEW/AGGREGATERATING — DO NOT FABRICATE AND DO NOT SELF-MARKUP: Per Google's review-snippet policy (reaffirmed Dec 2025), if the entity controls the reviews about itself, pages using LocalBusiness/Organization schema are INELIGIBLE for the review-star rich result — this self-serving rule applies even to embedded third-party widgets and self-collected testimonials. So do NOT add Review/AggregateRating schema describing Landmark on Landmark's own site, and never invent ratings or counts. Surface real reviews as on-page social proof (text/embeds) and drive review volume to the Google Business Profile, where stars surface through Google's own mechanism. Validate the whole graph in Google's Rich Results Test / Schema Markup Validator before launch.

### Technical-SEO checklist

- One self-referencing canonical (rel=canonical) per page; ensure dedicated material/service/city pages exist as real URLs (current 'Explore'/city links only jump to on-page anchors — replace with real page links)
- Clean, hierarchical URLs: /flooring/<material>/, /services/<service>/, /flooring/<city>/ (or /service-areas/<city>/), /commercial/, /property-management/, /financing/, /about/, /contact/, /reviews/
- XML sitemap listing every new page + submit in Google Search Console; add robots.txt referencing the sitemap
- Internal linking: homepage links to every material, service, segment and city page; material<->service<->city cross-links; breadcrumbs on every page (matches BreadcrumbList schema)
- Descriptive, keyword-relevant image alt text on every real photo (material + city/context); name files descriptively
- Core Web Vitals targets (p75): LCP < 2.5s, INP < 200ms, CLS < 0.1 — compress/serve next-gen images (WebP), set width/height to prevent layout shift, lazy-load below-fold media
- Mobile-first responsive layout; tap targets and readable font sizes (most local flooring traffic is mobile)
- Click-to-call: phone as tel:+17752973236 link in header and footer on every page
- Title tag 51–60 chars / <~580px and meta description ~150–158 chars / <~920px, unique per page, to reduce truncation and Google rewriting
- Single H1 per page; logical H2/H3 outline; title/H1/first-paragraph keyword coherence (material+city)
- Consistent NAP (name/address/phone) in footer site-wide, exactly matching the Google Business Profile
- Embed Google Map of the showroom and link the GBP; keep hours (Mon–Fri 9am–5pm) consistent across site + GBP
- HTTPS everywhere, no mixed content; 301-redirect any old single-page anchors to the new dedicated pages
- Validate structured data in Rich Results Test + Schema Markup Validator; monitor coverage in Search Console
