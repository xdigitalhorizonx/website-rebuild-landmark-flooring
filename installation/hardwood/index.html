What's included in a hardwood install

A Landmark hardwood installation is a full-scope job, not just nailing down boards. From the first measurement to the final sweep, here's what our crews handle so you don't have to coordinate three different trades:

  • On-site measure & layout planning: we verify square footage, plan board direction, and map seams and transitions to doorways and sightlines.
  • Acclimation: your wood sits in the home to reach equilibrium with indoor humidity before a single plank goes down (more on why below).
  • Old-floor removal & haul-away: tear-out of existing carpet, vinyl or tile and disposal, when needed.
  • Subfloor prep: moisture testing, flattening and fastening so the new floor has a sound, level base.
  • Installation: nail-down, glue-down or floating depending on the product and subfloor.
  • Finishing details: baseboards or shoe molding, transition strips and thresholds, plus a thorough clean-up.

Most residential hardwood projects run a few days from start to finish depending on square footage, removal, stairs and whether the floor is site-finished. We'll give you a realistic timeline with your written estimate — no surprises mid-job.

Acclimation & the dry-climate factor

This is where a local installer matters most. Northern Nevada sits at high desert elevation, and our indoor air is dry for much of the year — single-digit and teens relative humidity is common through Carson City, Reno and the Carson Valley in winter, then swings up in summer. Wood is hygroscopic: it gives up moisture and shrinks when the air is dry, and takes it back and expands when it's humid. Skip the acclimation step in a climate like ours and you invite the classic failures — gaps between boards, cupping, and a floor that creaks or pulls at the edges a season after install.

Our crews acclimate every hardwood floor to the conditions inside your home, run moisture-meter readings on both the wood and the subfloor, and target the wood's installed moisture content to your normal indoor range — not the warehouse it shipped from. It's the single most overlooked step in a wood install, and it's exactly why a national big-box crew that drops planks the same morning they arrive often leaves Northern Nevada homeowners with gapping by the next dry spell. Solid vs. engineered: install differences

How we fasten your floor depends on what you've chosen and what's under it. Both can look identical once they're down; the difference is in the method and where each works best.

FactorSolid hardwoodEngineered hardwood
Typical methodNail- or staple-downGlue-down, float or nail
SubfloorWood subfloor preferredWood or concrete slab
Below grade / slabNot recommendedSuitable
Climate stabilityMoves more with humidityMore dimensionally stable
RefinishingMany times over its lifeDepends on wear-layer thickness

In our dry valleys, engineered hardwood's layered construction holds its shape better through the seasons, which is why we often recommend it for slab-on-grade homes and rooms with bigger humidity swings. Solid wood still shines in the right setting and can be refinished again and again. Either way, we'll spec the right method for your subfloor during the estimate.

Hardwood installation FAQ

How long does hardwood need to acclimate before installation?
It depends on the product and your home's conditions, but acclimation is never something we skip in Northern Nevada's dry air. We let the wood reach equilibrium with your indoor humidity and confirm it with moisture-meter readings on both the boards and the subfloor before we begin — that's what prevents seasonal gapping and cupping later.
Can you install hardwood over a concrete slab?
Engineered hardwood is well suited to slab-on-grade homes and can be glued or floated over concrete after moisture testing. Solid hardwood generally isn't recommended directly over slab or below grade. We'll check your subfloor during the estimate and spec the right product and method for it.
Do you remove and haul away my old floor?
Yes. Tear-out of existing carpet, vinyl or tile and haul-away of the debris is part of the scope when your project needs it. We'll include any removal in your written estimate so the price you see covers the full job.
Should I install hardwood or refinish what I have?
If your existing wood floor is structurally sound and has enough surface left, refinishing is usually far cheaper than replacing it. If it's damaged, too thin to sand, or you want a different species or layout, new installation makes more sense. See our hardwood refinishing service, and we'll give you a straight recommendation at your estimate.
