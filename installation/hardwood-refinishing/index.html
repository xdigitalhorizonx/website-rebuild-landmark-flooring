Real hardwood is one of the few floors you can renew instead of rip out. Underneath the dull, scuffed surface most homeowners want gone is solid, beautiful wood — it just needs the worn finish sanded off and a new protective coat applied. That's exactly what refinishing does, and it's some of the highest-value work we do for Northern Nevada homeowners: you keep the character and resale value of genuine wood, skip the cost and disruption of a full tear-out, and walk away with floors that look brand new. Landmark Flooring handles every step in-house, from the first cut of the sander to the final cured coat.

Signs your floors need refinishing

Wood floors usually tell you when their finish has given out — you just have to know what to look for. If two or more of these sound familiar, it's likely time to refinish rather than keep living on a failing surface:

  • A dull, gray or cloudy look that stays even after a thorough cleaning — the protective topcoat has worn through.
  • Fine scratches and scuff "traffic lanes" in hallways, kitchens and in front of sinks where foot traffic concentrates.
  • Dark water marks or stains around exterior doors, pet bowls or under windows where snowmelt has crept in.
  • Bare or splintering spots where the finish is gone entirely and the raw wood is exposed and absorbing dirt.
  • You simply want a new color — a lighter, more modern tone or a richer stain to match a remodel.

As a rule of thumb, solid hardwood benefits from a fresh refinish roughly every 7–10 years depending on traffic and pets. Catching it before the finish fully wears through protects the wood itself — once the raw surface starts soaking up moisture and grime, repairs get more involved.

Refinish vs. replace: which makes sense?

The big question most homeowners ask is whether to refinish or start over. In the large majority of cases, if the boards are structurally sound and have enough thickness left to sand, refinishing is dramatically cheaper than replacing — and it preserves the original wood that gives an older Carson City or Reno home its character. Replacement only wins when boards are cupped beyond recovery, water-damaged through the subfloor, or sanded so many times there's no usable wood left above the tongue.

As a rough industry benchmark, refinishing existing hardwood commonly runs in the range of $3–$8 per square foot, while a full tear-out and new hardwood installation can land closer to $8–$25+ per square foot installed. These are general industry estimates, not a Landmark quote — your floor's species, condition and stain choice all move the number, which is why our in-home estimates are free. For a deeper walk-through of the decision, read our refinish vs. replace hardwood floors guide, and if you decide new wood is the better path we can handle that too.

Refinishing is usually right when…

  • The wear is in the finish, not the wood itself
  • Boards are solid and have thickness left to sand
  • You want to keep original wood & resale value
  • You want a new stain color without a full remodel

Replacement may be smarter if…

  • Boards are cupped, buckled or water-damaged through
  • The floor has been sanded too many times already
  • You want a totally different species or plank width — see hardwood flooring

Our hardwood refinishing process

Refinishing is detailed, dusty work that lives and dies on preparation — which is why we never hand it to random subcontractors. Here is how a typical Landmark refinishing project runs:

1

Assess & prep

We inspect board thickness, gaps and damage, set repair expectations, then clear and protect the space and seal off adjoining rooms for dust control.

2

Sand to bare wood

Multiple progressively finer sanding passes remove the old finish and surface scratches, with edgers reaching along walls and into corners for an even result.

3

Stain (optional)

Keep the natural tone or choose a new stain color. We can apply sample patches on your actual floor so you approve the shade before we commit.

4

Seal, finish & cure

Several coats of durable protective finish go down with buffing between coats. We'll walk you through dry times and how soon furniture and rugs can return.

Dust is the part homeowners worry about most, and modern equipment with vacuum-assisted containment keeps it far better controlled than the open sanding of decades past. We'll give you realistic dry and cure times up front: surfaces are typically walkable within hours of the final coat, but rugs and heavy furniture should wait days while the finish fully hardens — we'll set clear expectations for your specific finish before we start.

Refinishing & Northern Nevada's dry climate

Our high-desert climate matters for refinishing, not just for new installs. Carson City, Reno and the surrounding valleys sit at elevation with very low humidity, big day-to-night temperature swings, and dry forced-air heat all winter — conditions that pull moisture out of wood and open up seasonal gaps between boards. Light gapping is normal here and not something refinishing alone "fixes," but a fresh, properly cured finish helps seal the surface and slow that moisture exchange. We also let finishes cure on the right schedule for our dry air rather than rushing them, so the new coat bonds and hardens the way it should. It's the kind of local detail a crew that works these valleys every week understands — and a national franchise often doesn't.

Can engineered hardwood be refinished?

Sometimes — it depends entirely on the wear layer. Engineered hardwood is a real-wood top layer bonded over a stable plywood or composite core, and only that top veneer can be sanded. Thicker engineered products (roughly 3mm or more of solid wear layer) can usually be sanded and refinished at least once, much like solid wood. Thinner "click" engineered floors with a paper-thin veneer generally cannot be sanded without cutting through to the core, and are better refreshed by other means or replaced. If you're not sure what you have, we'll check the wear layer during your free estimate and tell you honestly whether refinishing is realistic. For background on solid versus engineered construction, see our hardwood flooring page.

Hardwood refinishing FAQ

How long does refinishing take?
Most single-area refinishing projects take a few days from the first sanding pass to a finish that's ready for normal use, depending on square footage, whether you're staining, and the finish system. We'll give you a realistic timeline — including dry and cure times — in your free estimate so you can plan around it.
Is hardwood refinishing really cheaper than replacing?
In most cases, yes — meaningfully so. As a general industry benchmark, refinishing existing wood often runs about $3–$8 per square foot versus roughly $8–$25+ per square foot to tear out and install new hardwood. Those are estimates, not a Landmark quote; condition, species and stain choice all affect the final number. See our refinish vs. replace guide.
How much dust does sanding create?
Far less than it used to. We use dust-containment equipment and seal off adjoining rooms, so the mess stays contained to the work area. Some fine dust is unavoidable with any sanding, but it's a small fraction of the open-sanding jobs of years past.
Can I change the color of my floors?
Absolutely — refinishing is the ideal time to go lighter, darker or richer. Because we sand back to bare wood, we can apply a new stain before sealing. We can place sample patches on your actual floor so you approve the exact shade before we coat the whole room.
