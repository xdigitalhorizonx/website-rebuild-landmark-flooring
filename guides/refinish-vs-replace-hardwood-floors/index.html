Refinish vs. Replace Hardwood Floors: Cost, Signs & What to Choose

The short answer: if your hardwood is structurally sound and still has enough wood left to sand, refinish it — it costs a fraction of replacement and looks brand-new. Replace only when the boards are too thin, water-damaged, rotted, or you want a different floor entirely. Here's how to tell which camp your floors fall into.

Deciding between refinish vs. replace for hardwood floors is one of the most common questions we hear from Carson City and Northern Nevada homeowners — usually right after they've pulled up an old carpet and found real wood underneath, or noticed their once-glossy floors have gone gray and worn. The good news is that most older hardwood floors are excellent candidates for refinishing, which is far cheaper and less disruptive than tearing them out. This guide walks you through a quick decision framework, real cost ranges (clearly labeled as estimates), the wear signs to look for, and the one factor that catches people off guard with engineered wood.

Quick decision framework

Before you get into the weeds, run your floors through these three questions. They'll point you toward the right answer in about two minutes.

  1. Is the floor solid wood (or engineered with a thick wear layer), and is it structurally sound? If yes, refinishing is almost always on the table.
  2. Is there enough wood left above the tongue-and-groove to sand? Solid hardwood can typically be sanded and refinished multiple times over its life; if it's already been sanded paper-thin, it may be time to replace.
  3. Is the damage cosmetic or structural? Surface scratches, dull finish, light stains and minor gaps are cosmetic — refinish. Rot, deep water swelling, buckling, pet-urine penetration into the wood, or large missing sections lean toward replacement.

In plain terms: refinish if the wood is healthy and just looks tired; replace if the wood itself is failing or you want a completely different material or layout. If you're not sure which bucket you're in, that's exactly what a free in-home assessment is for — we'll check board thickness and subfloor condition and tell you honestly.

Cost comparison: refinish vs. replace

Cost is usually the deciding factor, and the gap is wide. Refinishing reuses the wood you already own, so you're paying mostly for labor and finish — not new material, tear-out and disposal. Signs your hardwood needs refinishing

You don't need to refinish on a schedule — you refinish when the floor tells you it's time. As a rough rule, solid hardwood gets a sand-and-refinish every 7–10 years in a busy home, but the signs matter more than the calendar. Look for two or more of these:

Refinish when you see…

  • A dull, gray or patchy finish, especially in walkways
  • Surface scratches and scuffs that don't reach the bare wood
  • Light water marks or cupping that has flattened back out
  • You want a different stain color on the same boards

Lean toward replacing when…

  • Boards are cracked, splintering or have moved/buckled
  • Deep water damage, soft spots, rot or subfloor issues
  • The wood has been sanded so many times it's too thin to sand again
  • You want a different material — compare hardwood or luxury vinyl

A quick at-home test: drip a little water in a worn, high-traffic spot. If it beads up, the finish is still doing its job; if it soaks in and darkens the wood, the protective coat is gone and it's time to refinish before the bare wood starts taking damage.

A Northern Nevada note: gaps and our dry climate

One thing we see constantly in the Carson Valley, Reno and around Tahoe: thin seasonal gaps between solid hardwood boards in winter, when our high-desert humidity drops and the wood shrinks. Those gaps usually close back up in summer and, on their own, are not a reason to replace a floor — they're a normal response to a dry climate, and refinishing won't (and shouldn't) eliminate them. If your only complaint is seasonal hairline gapping on an otherwise solid floor, refinishing is still the right call. If gaps are permanent and wide, the wood has been damaged, or you're choosing a new floor, our best flooring for Northern Nevada's climate guide explains why engineered wood and luxury vinyl tend to stay more stable here.

Can engineered hardwood be refinished?

Sometimes — and this is where homeowners get caught out. Engineered hardwood is a real-wood veneer bonded over a plywood or HDF core, so whether it can be refinished depends entirely on the thickness of that top wear layer. A thick wear layer (roughly 2mm or more, often 3–6mm on premium products) can usually be sanded and refinished once, sometimes twice. A thin wear layer (around 0.6–1mm, common on budget engineered floors) generally cannot be sanded without cutting through to the core, so those floors are replaced rather than refinished. If you're not sure what you have, we can measure an inconspicuous edge or a vent cutout to check before recommending anything.

Our recommendation, simply

For the majority of solid-hardwood floors we look at in Northern Nevada, refinishing is the smarter spend: it restores the floor for a fraction of replacement cost, keeps the original character of older homes, and is far less disruptive. Replacement makes sense when the wood is genuinely failing, the wear layer is too thin to sand, or you're ready for a different floor altogether. Either way, the decision really comes down to two measurements — board/wear-layer thickness and the extent of damage — which is exactly what we check, for free, in your home.

Refinish vs. replace FAQ

How many times can hardwood be refinished?
Solid hardwood (typically 3/4" thick) can usually be sanded and refinished several times across its lifespan, because there's plenty of wood above the tongue-and-groove. Engineered wood depends on its wear-layer thickness — often once, sometimes twice for premium products, and not at all for thin budget veneers.
Is it cheaper to refinish or replace hardwood floors?
When refinishing is viable, it's almost always cheaper — often a third to half the cost of replacement — because you keep your existing wood and skip tear-out, disposal and new material. Replacement only becomes the better value when the wood is damaged or too thin to refinish. (Figures here are industry estimates, not a Landmark quote.)
Will refinishing fix gaps between my boards?
Refinishing can fill very minor gaps during the process, but seasonal hairline gaps in our dry Northern Nevada winters are normal and usually close in summer — they're not a defect and not a reason to replace. Permanently wide gaps or moved boards point to a wood or subfloor issue we'd assess in person.
How long does refinishing take, and can I stay home?
A typical refinish runs a few days including sanding, staining and curing time, versus a longer tear-out-and-install for replacement. We'll walk you through dust control, dry/cure times and which rooms to avoid during your estimate so you can plan around it.
