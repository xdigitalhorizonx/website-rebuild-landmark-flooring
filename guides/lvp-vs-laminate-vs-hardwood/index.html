LVP vs. Laminate vs. Hardwood: Which Flooring Is Right for Your Home?

When you're weighing LVP vs. laminate vs. hardwood, the honest answer is that one of them is almost certainly the best fit for your rooms, your budget and our dry high-desert climate — you just need a straight comparison to see which. This guide gives you that: a quick recommendation up front, a side-by-side table, and a plain-English breakdown of when each floor wins, from the Carson City crew that sells and installs all three.

The quick answer

If you want a single recommendation, here it is for most Northern Nevada homes:

  • Choose LVP (luxury vinyl plank) if you want one floor that does almost everything — waterproof, scratch-resistant, kid- and pet-proof, and stable through Carson City's dry winters. It's the floor we recommend most often for whole-home installs and busy households.
  • Choose laminate if you love a crisp wood look and want to stretch a tight budget in dry rooms — bedrooms, living rooms, hallways — where standing water isn't a daily risk.
  • Choose hardwood if you want genuine wood underfoot, the strongest long-term resale value, and a floor you can sand and refinish for decades — ideally engineered hardwood, which handles our low-humidity climate far better than solid planks.

Still torn between two of them? Keep reading — the table below and the "when to choose" sections will settle it. And when you're ready for an exact number on your home, our free estimate is the only quote that actually reflects your rooms and subfloor.

When to choose LVP (luxury vinyl plank)

Luxury vinyl is the most-requested floor in our Carson City showroom, and it's usually the right call when you want one durable, low-maintenance surface for most of the house. A quality rigid-core (SPC/WPC) LVP is genuinely waterproof, so it belongs in kitchens, bathrooms, laundry rooms, basements and mudrooms where neither laminate nor hardwood is at home. The thick wear layer shrugs off claws, dropped toys and grit tracked in from the trail, and the plank barely moves as humidity swings from arid summer afternoons to freezing winter nights.

Pick LVP if you have pets or young kids, want a true whole-home floor, or are turning a rental and need durability without the upkeep. The main trade-offs: it can't be refinished (you replace it when it eventually wears), and purists may still prefer the feel of real wood. If that's you, hardwood is the better fit. Otherwise, see everything LVP offers on our luxury vinyl flooring page.

When to choose laminate

Laminate is the value pick: a crisp, printed wood look bonded to a dense fiberboard core, finished with a tough wear surface that handles daily foot traffic well. It costs less than most LVP and real wood, which makes it a smart choice for bedrooms, living rooms, home offices and hallways — the dry rooms where you want the warmth of a wood look without the price tag.

The catch is water. Laminate's core is wood-based, so even on the newer "water-resistant" and "waterproof" lines, a flooded bathroom or a slow dishwasher leak can swell the planks at the seams. We steer customers who need true waterproofing toward LVP and keep laminate in the dry zones where it shines. If a budget-friendly wood look is your priority, compare options on our laminate flooring page.

When to choose hardwood

Nothing replicates the depth, grain and warmth of real wood, and nothing else can be sanded and refinished again and again — which is why hardwood still carries the strongest resale value of the three and can last generations. Choose it when you want a genuine, timeless floor for living rooms, dining rooms, bedrooms and formal spaces, and you're prepared to keep water and grit in check.

In Northern Nevada there's an important wrinkle: our bone-dry air pulls moisture out of solid wood, which can leave gaps between boards by late winter. That's why we usually recommend engineered hardwood here — its cross-layered construction stays far more dimensionally stable in low humidity. Proper acclimation and install matter even more in this climate. Explore species and finishes on our hardwood flooring page, and if you already have wood floors, see whether you should refinish or replace them.

A note on Carson City's high-desert climate

Climate is the factor national comparison articles miss, and it's exactly where a local crew earns its keep. Carson City, Reno and the Carson Valley sit in a dry, high-desert basin with very low indoor humidity for much of the year and big temperature swings between day and night. That environment is hard on flooring that moves with moisture: solid hardwood shrinks and gaps, and wet laminate swells.

That's why our default recommendations here lean toward engineered hardwood and waterproof LVP — both stay stable when the air is dry, and LVP also handles the snowmelt and mud of a Tahoe-area mudroom. If your project is near the lake, where moisture and seasonal cabins change the math, the right answer can shift again. We dig into all of this in our best flooring for Northern Nevada's climate guide.

A simple way to decide

If you're still on the fence, walk through it in this order:

  1. Does the room get wet? Kitchens, baths, basements and mudrooms → LVP. Dry rooms keep all three options open.
  2. What's the budget? Tightest budget for a wood look in dry rooms → laminate. Mid-range, do-it-all → LVP. Premium and long-term value → hardwood.
  3. Do you want real wood and refinishability? Yes → engineered hardwood. No → LVP or laminate.
  4. Pets, kids or a rental? Lean LVP for its waterproof, scratch-tough surface — see our best flooring for pets and kids notes.

Most Northern Nevada homes land on LVP for the wet and high-traffic zones and engineered hardwood where they want the real thing — and we're happy to lay samples of all three side by side in the showroom so you can see and feel the difference before you choose.

Comparison FAQ

Is LVP better than laminate?
For most homes, yes — LVP is waterproof and a bit more comfortable underfoot, while laminate is only water-resistant. Laminate's edge is price: it's often the cheaper way to get a wood look in dry rooms. If water is anywhere in the equation, we recommend LVP.
Which lasts longer — vinyl, laminate or hardwood?
Hardwood, by a wide margin, because it can be refinished and last for decades or longer. LVP typically lasts about 15–25 years and laminate around 10–20 before you replace them. The trade-off is that wood needs more care and the right climate handling.
What's the best flooring for Northern Nevada's dry climate?
Engineered hardwood and waterproof rigid-core LVP both stay stable in our low-humidity, high-desert air. Solid hardwood can gap in dry winters and laminate can swell if it gets wet, so we usually point homeowners toward engineered wood or LVP. More detail is in our climate guide.
Can any of these be refinished?
Only hardwood. Solid and most engineered hardwoods can be sanded and refinished (engineered depends on wear-layer thickness). LVP and laminate are replaced rather than refinished when they eventually wear out.
