Solid vs. engineered hardwood (and our dry-climate angle)

The most important decision you'll make isn't the species or the stain — it's solid versus engineered, and here that choice is tied directly to climate. Carson City and the surrounding valleys sit at roughly 4,700 feet in a high desert where relative humidity routinely drops into the teens and swings hard between bone-dry summer afternoons and freezing winter nights. Solid wood is a single piece of lumber: it drinks in and gives up moisture with the air, so in chronically dry interiors it can shrink and open up gaps between boards, and after a humid spell it can cup. We see this all the time on floors that were installed without respect for the local environment.

Engineered hardwood solves most of that. It's built from a real-wood wear layer bonded over a cross-layered plywood or composite core, and that construction resists the seasonal expansion and contraction that plagues solid planks in arid rooms. The top is still genuine hardwood — it looks identical underfoot — but it stays far more dimensionally stable. For most Northern Nevada homes, especially over concrete slabs or with radiant heat, we most often recommend engineered hardwood. Solid wood is still a wonderful choice in the right room with proper humidity management; we'll tell you honestly which fits your home. Either way, correct acclimation on site before installation is non-negotiable, and it's a big reason our floors stay flat. Species, finishes & looks

Once the construction is settled, the fun begins. Hardwood comes in a range of species, each with its own hardness and grain personality:

  • Oak — the regional favorite. White and red oak are durable, take stain beautifully, and their open grain hides everyday wear, which makes oak forgiving in active households.
  • Hickory — one of the hardest domestic woods, with dramatic grain and color variation; an excellent pick for high-traffic homes and anyone who loves a rustic, characterful look.
  • Maple — smoother, more uniform grain and a clean, contemporary feel; lighter and subtler than oak or hickory.

You'll also choose between site-finished wood (sanded and finished in your home for a seamless, sealed surface and unlimited stain colors) and pre-finished wood (factory-coated for a tougher, faster, lower-dust install). Add to that your pick of plank widths — wide planks read modern and upscale — plus matte, satin or wire-brushed textures. Hardwood flooring FAQ

Does hardwood survive Northern Nevada's dry climate?
Yes — when it's the right product, properly acclimated and installed. Our high-desert air is dry enough that solid wood can gap or cup if it isn't planned for, which is why we most often recommend engineered hardwood here and always acclimate the wood on site first. With those steps, a real-wood floor holds up beautifully in Carson City, Reno and the Carson Valley.
Should I choose solid or engineered hardwood?
For most local homes — especially over concrete slabs or with radiant heat — engineered hardwood is the more stable, lower-risk choice in our dry climate. Solid wood is still excellent in the right room with humidity management. Both have genuine hardwood surfaces and look identical underfoot; we'll recommend the right one for your specific space in your free estimate.
Can engineered hardwood be refinished?
Often, yes — it depends on the thickness of the real-wood wear layer. Thicker wear layers can be sanded and refinished one or more times; very thin ones cannot. If you're weighing your options, see our refinish vs. replace guide or ask us to inspect your floor.
Where should I avoid hardwood?
We generally steer homeowners away from solid hardwood in bathrooms, laundry rooms and below-grade basements where standing water and moisture are a risk. For those spaces, waterproof luxury vinyl is usually the smarter call — and many homes pair hardwood in living areas with LVP in wet rooms.
